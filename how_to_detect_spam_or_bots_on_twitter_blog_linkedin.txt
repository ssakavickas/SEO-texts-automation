Most engagement metrics on Twitter are lying to you. Not occasionally. Consistently.

Independent researchers estimate that somewhere between 9% and 20% of active accounts are automated. Twitter's official number is 5%. The gap between those figures has never been resolved, and the platform hasn't released the data needed to settle it. What that means in practice: if you're collecting Twitter data for brand monitoring, research, or any kind of analysis, a meaningful slice of what you're measuring is noise generated by machines.

The frustrating part is that obvious bots are not the problem. The unsophisticated ones are easy to catch. The ones that actually distort your data are engineered to look human, posting at irregular intervals, maintaining plausible follower ratios, building history over months. No single detection method handles those reliably.

What works is layering. Follow/follower ratios, posting velocity, profile completeness, username patterns, network clustering, engagement mismatches. None of these signals are decisive alone. Combined, they give you something useful: a probabilistic filter that meaningfully improves data quality before your analysis even begins.

The mental shift that matters most is treating bot detection as a filter, not a verdict. You're not trying to prove an account is a bot. You're trying to keep your dataset clean enough that the numbers you report actually reflect reality.
