Most people treating competitor price tracking as a manual task have already lost before they start.

You visit five sites, log the numbers, and by the time you finish the sixth, the first three have changed. Scale that to hundreds of products across dozens of retailers and the whole exercise collapses under its own weight.

Automated price extraction is the obvious answer. But here is where most teams go wrong: they assume it is simple. Visit a page, find the price, record it. In practice, modern e-commerce sites load prices dynamically through JavaScript, meaning a basic HTTP request often returns a shell with no price data at all. Then there are anti-bot measures, inconsistent price formats, selector drift when sites redesign, and the quiet nightmare of a scraper that fails silently for days before anyone notices.

The approach you choose matters as much as the decision to automate. No-code tools work well for small catalogs and non-technical teams. Python-based pipelines give developers full control but come with serious maintenance overhead. Dedicated platforms like Prisync handle repricing logic but cost accordingly. Scraping APIs sit in the middle — low engineering effort, high reliability, good for scale.

The teams that do this well treat price extraction like any serious data pipeline: explicit failure handling, output validation, and monitoring that catches problems before they corrupt your dataset.
